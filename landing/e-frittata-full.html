TodayToday Both languages flip the same egg dish. Only one of them is accusing you.Spanish flips the tortilla and wins the match. Italian flips the frittata and dodges the blame. EspañolDar la vuelta a la tortillalit. “to flip the omelette”To completely reverse a situation, turning it in one's favor.Íbamos perdiendo 2-0, pero logramos dar la vuelta a la tortilla y ganamos el partido.We were losing 2-0, but we managed to turn things around and won the match. ItalianoRivoltare la frittatalit. “to flip the frittata”To spin a situation to make oneself look better; to twist an argument to dodge blame.Ha sbagliato lui, ma ha rivoltato la frittata e ora sembra colpa mia.He was the one who made the mistake, but he flipped the frittata and now it looks like it's my fault.

BridgeNearly identical cooking imagery — a flat egg dish being flipped — hiding a real divergence in meaning. Spanish 'dar la vuelta a la tortilla' is a genuine, often triumphant reversal of fortune; Italian 'rivoltare la frittata' is manipulative — someone distorting the story to dodge blame. The examples show it: Spain's is a comeback, Italy's is an accusation.

es → it Using 'rivoltare la frittata' for a genuine comeback misfires badly — you are accusing yourself of spin. For the sports-comeback sense Italian says 'ribaltare la situazione' or 'ribaltare il risultato'.

it → es 'Dar la vuelta a la tortilla' does not carry the blame-shifting accusation; a Spanish listener hears a neutral or positive turnaround. For manipulative spin Spanish reaches elsewhere (e.g. 'tergiversar').

YesterdayYesterday EspañolQuedarse de piedralit. “to remain of stone”To be stunned; frozen in shock.Cuando me lo contó, me quedé de piedra.When she told me, I was stunned. ItalianoRimanere di sassolit. “to remain of stone”To be left speechless with shock.Sono rimasto di sasso alla notizia.I was stunned at the news.

BridgeTwin idioms — both turn you to stone, both use a 'remain' verb.

es → it HIGH VALUE: rimanere takes essere — sono rimasto di sasso. Spanish speakers reach for 'ho rimasto'. This is a flagship interference entry.
